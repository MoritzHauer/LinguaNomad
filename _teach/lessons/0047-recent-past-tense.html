Lesson 0047

Recent Past — Eyewitness

You will be able to form the past tense for things you personally witnessed, and understand why Kyrgyz distinguishes eyewitness from hearsay.

Mission: communication Skill: grammar Time: 25–30 minutes

Builds on: Lesson 0033 — Verb Tenses, Lesson 0030 — Agglutination

The Eyewitness Past Tense

The eyewitness past (also called the simple past or direct past) marks events you personally saw, did, or experienced. The pattern is:

verb stem + -ды/-ди/-ту/-тү + personal ending

PersonEndingExample (бар- to go)English
мен-мБардымI went
сен-ңБардыңYou went (informal)
сиз-ңызБардыңызYou went (formal)
ал—БардыHe/she went
биз-кБардыкWe went
силер-ңарБардыңарYou all went
алар-штыБарыштыThey went

Choosing -ды or -ты

The connecting suffix changes depending on the final sound of the verb stem:

Final sound of stemSuffixExample
Voiced consonant or vowel-ды / -дибар → барды (voiced р)
Voiceless consonant (к, п, т, с, ш, ч, ф, х)-ту / -түкет → кетти (voiceless т, doubled)

When the stem ends in -т and the suffix is also -ту/-тү, the consonant doubles: кет + ти → кетти.

Eyewitness vs Hearsay: A Required Distinction

Kyrgyz grammar obliges you to encode how you know something. This is not optional politeness — it is a core grammatical category called evidentiality.

Evidential typePatternExampleMeaning
Eyewitness (you saw it)stem + -дыАл бардыHe went (I saw it)
Hearsay (you heard about it)stem + -ыптырАл барыптырThey say he went

Using the eyewitness past for something you only heard about is a factual error in Kyrgyz — like reporting hearsay as direct testimony.

Examples in Context

  • Мен базарга бардым. — I went to the bazaar. (I know because I was there.)
  • Апам кечке чейин иштеди. — My mother worked until evening. (I witnessed it.)
  • Жолдошум барыптыр. — Apparently my friend went. (I only heard this.)

Evidentiality is grammatically obligatory

Many European languages leave the source of evidence implicit. In Kyrgyz, it is encoded in the verb ending itself. Every time you use the past tense, you make a claim about how you know. Choosing the wrong form is not just imprecise — it is grammatically incorrect. Learn to ask yourself: Did I personally witness this, or did I hear about it?
